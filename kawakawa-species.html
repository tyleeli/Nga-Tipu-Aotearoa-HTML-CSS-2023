Kawakawa species

Piper excelsum (formerly known as Macropiper excelsum) of the pepper family (Piperaceae) and commonly known as kawakawa, is a small tree of which the subspecies P. excelsum subsp. excelsum is endemic to New Zealand

Kawakawa is found throughout the North Island, and as far south as Okarito (43.20 °S) on the West Coast, and Banks Peninsula (43.5 °S) on the east coast of the South Island. The tree grows to 20 feet (6.1 m).

Quick facts

  • Kawakawa leaves are about 5–10 cm long by 6–12 cm wide
  • The leaves are often covered with holes caused by the caterpillar of the kawakawa looper moth.
  • The leaves are deep green in colour if growing in the forest, but may be yellowish-green in more open situations.
  • Kawakawa essential oil contains myristicin, a deliriant when consumed in high concentrations.

Kawakawa species

The name kawakawa comes from Te Reo, where it refers to the bitter taste of the leaves, from kawa or bitter.

  • Each berry cluster is the size of a small finger. Ripening period is January and February. These fruits are favoured by kererū or New Zealand pigeon (Hemiphaga novaeseelandiae) and tūī (Prosthemadera novaeseelandiae)
  • In cultural contexts, host people of a marae wave leaves of kawakawa to welcome guests.
  • At a tangi, both hosts and guests may wear wreaths of kawakawa on the head as a sign of mourning.
